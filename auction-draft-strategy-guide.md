# Timeless Auction Draft Strategy — Consolidated Notes

*Synthesized from 12 episodes of "The Auction Brief" podcast. Player names, prices, and season-specific takes have been stripped out — this is the durable strategic skeleton.*

---

## 1. Preparation: The Foundation

**The single highest-leverage piece of prep is the "par sheet."**
- Before the draft, write down an *exact dollar amount* you plan to spend at every roster spot (starters and bench), adding up to your full cap (e.g., exactly $200, not "about $200").
- As the draft proceeds, track a running tally of how far over/under "par" you are at each position (e.g., spent $5 over on RB1, $3 under on WR2 → net +$2 you can spend elsewhere).
- This converts a chaotic, emotional process into a simple tracking exercise: you always know exactly how much flexibility you have left.
- Make 2–3 *different* par sheets reflecting different strategic builds (e.g., "studs and duds," "balanced," "anchor RB") so you can pivot early based on how the room is playing.
- Percentage-based budgeting ("spend 40% of cap on RBs") is worse than exact dollar figures — percentages are hard to compute on the fly and don't tell you what to do in the moment.

**Tier sheets, not ranked lists.**
- Group players into tiers/clusters rather than a strict 1-2-3 ranking. Auction prices are not linear — players in the same tier can and should be treated as interchangeable.
- Knowing where tier breaks fall tells you when scarcity is about to spike a position's price.

**Average Auction Value (AAV) is much weaker than people think.**
- AAV is an average across thousands of leagues with different formats, settings, scoring rules, and roster constructions — almost no two leagues are alike, so a generic AAV number rarely matches what will actually happen in your specific room.
- AAV is *not* the same thing as ADP. ADP (snake draft position) is predictive — it tells you reliably when in the draft order a player will be taken. AAV is not predictive in the same way; a single aggressive bidder can blow it out of the water, or a room's overall lack of interest in a position can crater it.
- Best use of AAV: a rough orientation for total beginners. Beyond that, it can actively mislead you into bidding at the wrong moments or missing/overpaying based on a stale "deal" signal.
- What actually determines price in *your* room: your league's personality, individual managers' tendencies, positional scarcity at that moment, and how much cap is left in the room — not a number pulled from outside leagues.

**Know your league's personality before you draft.**
- Leagues (and especially recurring home leagues) develop consistent personalities: some chronically undervalue quarterbacks, some are rookie-crazy, some avoid unproven/new-situation players, some overpay for name-brand stars. Learn this ahead of time and build your par sheet around it rather than around generic market values.
- In a brand-new league of strangers, you can't know this in advance — but you can learn it live in the first 15–20% of nominations and adjust.

**"War-game" your draft ahead of time.**
- Mentally run through scenarios before you draft: "If Player A goes for more than $X, what's my Plan B?" "If I miss my top tight end target, who do I pivot to, and at what price?"
- Decide in advance how you'll react when your plan breaks (and it will). The goal isn't a perfect plan — it's training your brain to make quick, non-panicked decisions when the actual auction inevitably deviates from the script.
- Compare/contrast similarly-priced players ahead of time so you already know your preference order when they come up for bid — don't make these comparisons cold, in the moment.
- "Get greedy" in your pre-draft planning: imagine an aggressive, slightly unrealistic dream roster build. You probably won't get the exact players, but going through the exercise sharpens your sense of which value pockets to attack.

## 2. Nomination Strategy

**Every nomination needs a reason.** Never nominate a player just because "I like him" — that's not a strategy. Ask: does this nomination define my draft direction, drain the right kind of money from the room, attack an opponent's weakness, or protect my own plan?

**Don't nominate top-tier players you don't want, hoping to "waste people's money."**
- This doesn't work for two reasons:
 1. The math doesn't support it — pulling one big price out of a $2,000–3,000+ total-cap pool barely dents the market.
 2. The manager who lands that elite player didn't waste money — they got a great player. You haven't hurt them; you've just removed a player you can't have anyway.
- Worse, nominating that player gives the room *clarity* — once he's sold, everyone who wanted him is freed up to spend elsewhere with confidence. Leaving him on the board instead keeps multiple managers mentally "reserving" cap space for him, which is more valuable to you than the (illusory) gain from "wasting" his price.

**Early in the draft: define your draft, don't try to manipulate the market.**
- The first several nominations should be the "cornerstone" players who determine which direction/strategy your roster takes. You're not getting bargains on true elite, top-tier talent early — pay the going rate to find out whether you're landing your targets, then build around the answer.
- Don't bother trying to wait out elite players hoping the price will crater after many rounds — it essentially never happens; someone else nominates and buys them regardless of what you do.

**Middle of the draft: read the flow and adjust.**
- This is where "wasting" nominations (calling out players you don't want, in a hot market, to drain other people's cap) actually starts to work, because the room has differentiated into haves/have-nots.
- If the market is hot, nominate players you don't want — other people will overpay for them. If it's cold/cautious, nominate players you do want and try to land deals.
- Avoid the reflexive instinct of "I just spent big at a position, so let me throw out the next name at that position to drain people." It rarely has the effect you think — let the market's natural behavior do that work for you, and use your nominations on positions where the discount opportunity is real.
- Never nominate a player ranked far below where the market currently is ("reaching too deep" into your rankings) — when cap money is still plentiful, those players get bid up far past their fair value because the cost is "only a few dollars" to a manager flush with cash. Conversely, that same player nominated later (when money is scarcer) often goes for a fraction of the price.

**Late in the draft: precision matters enormously.**
- Don't sit on a player you want for too long, hoping for an ever-better price — eventually you run out of rounds, lose flexibility, or get stuck overpaying anyway because you waited too long to find out the actual cost. Nominate him, find out the price, and then make your decision with real information.
- Roster spots themselves become a form of currency late in the draft. Avoid getting stuck with a low-ranked "filler" player you don't want just because the price was cheap — an empty/flexible roster spot is often worth more than a marginal player.
- Conversely, you can deliberately nominate a marginal player you don't mind someone else having, to consume one of an opponent's remaining roster spots and reduce their flexibility.
- When cap amounts shrink to single dollars, know exactly who can bid more than $1 and who can't — nominating at the right opening price (e.g., starting at $2 instead of $1) can save money, because a $1 opener often becomes a $3 final price (everyone reflexively says "$2"), whereas a $2 opener frequently just holds.

**Don't be predictable.** Vary how/when you nominate "players you want" vs. "players to drain the room" vs. "stop-a-run" picks. If your league mates can predict your nomination pattern year after year, they will counter it.

## 3. Bidding Strategy

**Bid quickly and decisively rather than dragging out the clock.**
- Waiting until the last second (or letting an auctioneer get to "going twice…") doesn't intimidate opponents — it usually signals hesitation, which *encourages* others to keep bidding.
- It also gives opponents time to think, get comfortable with a high number, and calculate whether they can afford one more dollar. Fast, confident bidding compresses their decision window and induces mistakes.
- Beginners: stick to simple "+1" bids. There's no need to get fancy before you've mastered the basics.

**Market price enforcing vs. bidding someone up — know the difference.**
- *Market price enforcing*: bidding a player up to a price you'd genuinely be happy to pay, because the price is clearly too low for the room. You should be willing to actually take the player.
- *Bidding someone up / "sticking" an opponent*: deliberately running up the price on a player you don't want, targeted at a specific opponent's need or tendency, with real risk you get stuck with him.
- Don't *always* be the enforcer/sheriff in the room — it's high variance (you will eventually misjudge a situation and get stuck with an unwanted player), and once the room identifies you as "the enforcer," they'll stop bidding and let you do the work, which removes your ability to pick off value.
- Let other deals happen without jumping in on every single one. You cannot defend every fair price; passing on some bargains is necessary discipline.
- Calibrate this like a poker calling-range: if you're never "wrong" — i.e., you only ever land players you deliberately wanted at exactly the price you expected — you're probably playing too conservatively and leaving value on the table. You should periodically end up with players you didn't expect to draft, because the price was simply too good to pass up.

**Specific bidding tactics (use selectively, not on every player):**
- *Jump bid / "name your number" bid*: Instead of incrementing one dollar at a time on a player you've already decided you're willing to pay up to $X for, just say $X (or close to it) immediately. This puts maximum pressure on opponents with minimal time to think, and often ends the bidding faster/cheaper than a slow walk-up would.
- *Precision "showstopper" bid*: When bidding has stalled a few dollars below where you think a player is truly worth, instead of incrementing by $1, jump several dollars at once — especially across a psychological round-number barrier (e.g., from the $30s into the $40s). This forces your opponent's next bid to also cross that barrier, which carries extra psychological weight and often ends the auction outright.
- *Stop-and-go*: Bid actively early on a player you want, then deliberately go silent for a stretch (even though you intend to come back in). This can draw out other interested bidders who think you've dropped out, and lets you re-enter late with a surprise bid that is maximally deflating to whoever thought they'd already won.
- *Plus-two instead of plus-one*: In a heads-up bidding war, if your opponent has shown any hesitation on their last bid, jumping by two dollars instead of one forces them to clear an extra psychological hurdle to continue (e.g., they have to go from $23 to $26 instead of $24) and can end the bidding faster.
- *The "silent sniper"*: Watch two other managers slug it out over a higher-priced player without participating at all, then jump in late with one bid right before time expires. Often wins the player cheaply because the other two are emotionally and financially spent by that point.
- General principle behind all of these: *more total bids on a player generally drive the price higher.* Staying out of a bidding war entirely (when you don't actually want the player) is itself a form of restraint that benefits the market and, ultimately, you.

**Push the action — get prices to the "right neighborhood" quickly.** If you know a player should clear $30, don't let the bidding crawl up one dollar at a time while everyone deliberates — jump it into that range immediately so the room reacts to reality rather than slowly discovering it.

## 4. Stages of the Draft / "Inflection Points"

An auction isn't one continuous process — it moves through distinct phases, and your strategy should shift as you move through them. Recognizing *when the shift is happening* is one of the most valuable skills you can develop.

**How to detect a shift:** Watch for an unusually high or unusually low price relative to recent comparable players, or an unusual/unexpected nomination (e.g., someone reaching far down the rankings). Either is a signal that the room's behavior is changing.

**Typical phases:**
1. **The "sitting down bonus" (very early).** At the start of a draft, many rooms are cautious/cold — people are reluctant to commit large chunks of their cap right away. This can last anywhere from a couple of nominations to almost a full round. If you sense this, be ready to jump on a near-top-tier player at a modest discount, because true top-tier prices rarely crater later.
2. **Elite-player feeding frenzy.** Once the room "warms up" (you'll see a player go for full or above-market price), the next several rounds are dominated by top-tier talent going at or above fair value, with heavy competition. You will not find bargains here — your only job is to land one or two of the players central to your build and accept paying close to market.
3. **The value window.** As the very top tiers get exhausted but plenty of cap is still in the room, a window opens where good (but not elite) players go for genuine discounts, because remaining bidders are starting to get cautious/patient while waiting for who's left. This window tends to be short (a round or two) — move decisively to grab value here.
4. **"Get your guy" mode.** Once most of the truly impactful players are gone, the focus shifts: managers stop chasing best-available talent and instead hunt specifically for personal favorites/depth fits, regardless of market price. Counterintuitively, this causes the last few remaining "name" players to spike in price (a handful of bidders all want the *same* dwindling supply) while everyone else gets cheap. By this stage you want your roster's core mostly finished — being caught needing a "name" player here is expensive.
5. **High competition / low cap squeeze.** Near the very end, several managers may have only modest cap left but they're competing hard for the last few decent players. Don't assume low remaining dollar totals guarantee bargains — this phase is often *more* inflationary on a per-player basis than the value window was, because there's nothing else left to spend the money on.
6. **Dollar-bill endgame.** In the final stretch, most nominations sell for $1–4. Keep a couple of dollars in reserve so you're not stuck only able to bid $1 (where any competing bid of $2 eliminates you outright). Pay attention to who can afford to bid past $1 and who can't.

**General lesson:** your nomination and bidding strategy should be different in each phase. Plan your "core" purchases for phases 2–3, hold disposable nominations for phase 4, and protect roster flexibility heading into phases 5–6.

## 5. Reading the Room: Psychology & Tells

**Auctions are a human/game-theory exercise as much as a fantasy-football exercise.** Two complementary lenses:
- *Game theory:* why would a rational manager be bidding/nominating right now, given their roster needs and remaining cap?
- *Human psychology:* body language, team allegiances, drinking, excitement level, frustration — these leak real information.

**Watch for nomination and bidding "tells."**
- Some managers only bid when they actually want the player (their bidding behavior directly reveals intent).
- Some only nominate players they personally want; others only nominate players they don't want, to test the market.
- Bidding cadence changes (someone who bids instantly vs. only late) can reveal whether they're truly engaged on a given player.
- A manager who goes uncharacteristically quiet, or stops chatting/joking right as a player comes up, may be signaling real interest.
- "Bad" or inexperienced players assume everyone else is constantly bluffing/scheming; better players recognize that, most of the time, *the thing is the thing* — straightforward behavior usually means exactly what it looks like. Reserve suspicion of trickery for managers you have actual evidence are sophisticated enough to deploy it.

**Manage your own table image.**
- Being personable, good-humored, and easy to be around in the room is not just etiquette — it's strategically useful. Opponents who like you are less likely to deliberately target you with "stick" bids; opponents who think you're a pushover, however, may try, so you still need the willingness to enforce a price occasionally to keep that reputation in check.
- Early in the draft, project aggression and unpredictability (bid actively, vary your patterns) even before you've locked up much of your roster — this builds a "table image" of being engaged and dangerous, which makes opponents cautious around you later without requiring you to have actually spent much.
- Conversely, staying quiet for long stretches is a legitimate and useful tool, especially as the draft progresses — silence doesn't cost you anything, and it lets others make spending mistakes you don't have to clean up.

**Identify the "bully"/"table captain"** — the manager trying to dominate the room's tempo with aggressive spending — and use targeted nominations/bids to keep them in check rather than letting them run the draft uncontested. Do this without becoming a bully yourself; the goal is control, not domination.

**Anticipate irrational behavior.** Managers will sometimes bid or spend in ways that don't make logical sense given their roster — they may blow a large chunk of cap on one player rather than spreading it across needs, simply because of in-the-moment impulse. Don't assume people will always "play it safe" with remaining dollars; plan for the possibility that they won't, and don't bank on a deal materializing just because, on paper, an opponent "shouldn't" be able to afford to compete.

## 6. Mindset, Discipline, and Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Be mostly "player agnostic."** The large majority of the player pool should be treated as interchangeable value propositions, not players you have a personal attachment to. Reserve genuine, must-have conviction for a small handful of targets. This makes it far easier to walk away from bad prices rather than getting emotionally pulled into overpaying.

**Don't force a plan or a specific player onto your roster.**
- Auctions punish forcing. If you decide pre-draft "I must have Player X," you either end up waiting too long (losing pivot options) or overpaying (or both).
- Accept up front that you will be wrong about players constantly, and that the most attractive deals of the draft will often land on players you didn't expect, weren't targeting, or don't even especially like. If you never end up with at least a couple of "value pickups" you didn't plan for, you're probably not exploiting the market enough.

**Have variety / be unpredictable across the whole draft, not just bidding.** Vary your nomination timing, bidding cadence, and overall approach round to round. Predictable behavior (e.g., always opening with the same type of player, always reaching the same way) gets exploited by anyone who's seen you draft before.

**Maintain a "killer mentality," especially late.** Auction drafts are direct, head-to-head conflict in a way snake drafts are not — you can actively prevent an opponent from getting a player simply by bidding. Treat the room as competitive throughout, but particularly in the closing stretch: finishing the last 20–30% of the draft strongly (rather than coasting once you're "satisfied enough") is frequently the difference between a good team and a championship-caliber one.

**Don't worry about how your final prices "look."** The goal of an auction is to assemble the best possible roster, not to be able to brag afterward about getting bargains on every player. If you're forced to overpay for a player who fills a real need or represents real remaining talent, do it — a few "wasted" dollars matter far less than an empty or weak roster spot.

**Self-scout relentlessly.** Because you control your own habits more reliably than you can read an entire room of opponents, the fastest path to improvement is reviewing your own draft afterward: where did you waste a nomination, panic-bid, or freeze up? Treat every completed auction as film study.

**Watch for the "magnification" effect.** Public sentiment about certain players compounds in an auction in a way it doesn't in a snake draft — minor doubts repeated by many sources all summer get baked into a uniformly depressed price, even disproportionate to the actual downside risk. Identify players who seem to be suffering from broad, repeated (but not necessarily well-founded) negative sentiment; these are frequently underpriced relative to a snake-draft equivalent, because in a snake draft a player still gets picked "in his slot" regardless of sentiment, whereas in an auction sentiment directly suppresses price.

**Recognize when "the market is just right" and there's no exploitable mistake to find.** Sometimes a room full of sharp drafters will price things accurately and there's no edge to find beyond a few marginal dollars. In those rooms, the win comes from disciplined execution (nominations, par-sheet management, endgame play) rather than finding mispriced players.

## 7. Roster Construction Philosophy

**Decide on a build philosophy before the draft, then let the room confirm or redirect it.** Common archetypes referenced repeatedly: a "stars and scrubs" approach (concentrate cap in a few elite players, fill out the rest cheaply), a "balanced" approach (no true stars, no real weaknesses), an "anchor" approach at a given position (one strong, expensive player at a position, then deliberately go cheap/volume at the rest of that position), or going unusually heavy/light at a specific position relative to convention.

**The depth/upside tradeoff is real and worth thinking about explicitly.**
- Rosters built entirely around "floor" depth (lots of solid-but-unspectacular players at the back of the roster) can feel safe but tend to cap your ceiling — if your shallow bench players underperform, there's no equivalent upside elsewhere to compensate.
- Pushing more cap toward the top of the roster (fewer, better difference-makers) increases variance but also increases ceiling; if you've been consistently building overly cautious, "deep-but-flat" rosters, deliberately experimenting with spending more aggressively at the top is worth trying.
- In formats with very deep rosters/lots of bench or flex spots, "playable depth" (players who can be confidently started, not lottery-ticket flyers) is valuable — but don't let chasing that depth crowd out your ability to also build a genuinely high-ceiling top of the roster.

**Auction drafts allow roster constructions impossible in a snake draft.** You can deliberately concentrate spending in one area (e.g., several mid-tier players at one position rather than the standard one-per-round spread) because you're not constrained by "my turn comes once every 12 picks." Take advantage of this flexibility rather than defaulting to a snake-draft mental model of "next best player available."

**Don't apply snake-draft ("linear") thinking to auctions.** In a snake draft, players are drafted in a roughly fixed relative order regardless of room. In an auction, price is *not* fixed relative order — a player nominally "worse" than another can legitimately cost more, depending on what's left on the board, who still needs that position, and how much cap remains in the room at that moment. Decisions should be driven by the situation in front of you, not by an assumed ranking order.

## 8. A Simple Mental Checklist for In-the-Moment Decisions

When a player comes up for bid, the recurring set of questions worth running through (consciously at first, automatically with experience):
- What's my roster situation — what do I still need, and how badly?
- What's my remaining cap, and what's left on my par sheet for this slot?
- What's the opponent's roster situation and remaining cap (and can they even afford to keep bidding)?
- How deep is the remaining pool at this position/tier (is scarcity about to spike)?
- What stage of the draft are we in (early frenzy, value window, get-your-guy mode, endgame)?
- Is this nomination/bid event itself signaling a shift I should react to?
- Are there human/psychological tells from specific opponents relevant here?

Putting these together quickly and instinctively — rather than treating each as a separate, effortful calculation — is what separates an experienced auction drafter from a beginner. It comes only from repetition: doing real auctions (live ones, when possible, since they reveal more behavioral information than online drafts), and reviewing them afterward.
